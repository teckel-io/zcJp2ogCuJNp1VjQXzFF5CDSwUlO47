User Guide

Benedict — User Guide & Best Practices

Benedict is your voice compliance assistant. He listens, fills in your forms, shows them on screen, and emails the finished PDF when you’re done. He’s quick, usually inexpensive, and a few simple habits will keep every session accurate, fast, and cheap.

Getting started

Open your practice’s Benedict page in your browser. You’ll see two things side by side:

  • On the left: the Benedict voice widget — your way to talk to him.
  • On the right: the form viewer (currently empty) and a running cost meter at the top.

To start, click the Benedict widget once and allow microphone access if your browser asks. Then just speak — there’s no special wake word. Try “hi Benedict”, “I want to fill in a form”, or “open the Crown consent.”

A typical session, end to end

  1. Greet Benedict. “Hi Benedict” or “I want to work on a form.” He’ll respond and ask what you’d like to do.
  2. Find your form. Either say a phrase from the form’s title (“Crown consent”, “cleaning specification”, “patient survey”) and Benedict will search the catalogue, OR ask “what forms do I have?” to hear the list.
  3. Start filling. Once Benedict has the form, he’ll walk through it question-by-question — OR you can volunteer many answers in one breath. Volunteering is recommended; it’s faster and cheaper.
  4. Watch the form fill in real time. Ask “show me the form” to open the viewer on the right. You’ll see each answer appear in the PDF as you give it.
  5. Make corrections any time. Tell Benedict “change question 7 to no” or “the answer to age is 34.” He’ll overwrite the previous value.
  6. Skip PII when you’d rather not share it. If a question asks for a personal detail (patient name, date of birth, contact info) and you’d prefer to leave it blank, say “skip.” Benedict will leave that field blank and continue. The form will still submit.
  7. Submit when you’re ready. When all required fields are filled, Benedict will offer to submit. Say “yes please” or “submit” and he’ll email a PDF copy to the recipient on file.

Speaking to Benedict — what works

  • Be natural. Whole sentences, fragments, “yeah” / “yep” / “no thanks” — all fine. He’s not a command line.
  • Batch your answers. “Yes to 1, 2 and 4; no to 3; the rest are all acknowledged.” → one save, less waiting. This is the biggest single thing you can do to speed up a session.
  • Use a question number OR a content cue. “Set question 12 to acknowledged” and “the one about anaesthetic should be local” both work. Use whichever feels more natural.
  • Ask for a review any time. “Review my entries” → he’ll read back everything you’ve filled so far.
  • Pause whenever you need to. Long pauses cost very little — far less than active conversation. Take a phone call, look up a record, come back — Benedict will be waiting.
  • Don’t whisper. Speak clearly at normal volume; Benedict transcribes a clear signal better than a quiet one.

Resuming a form you didn’t finish

If you started a form earlier and didn’t finish, say “resume the Crown consent” or “show my in-progress forms.” Benedict will pick up where you left off — your previous answers are preserved. You don’t need to restate anything you’ve already answered.

Surveys (multiple respondents in one log)

For a Patient Satisfaction Survey or similar log-style form, Benedict will ask for one respondent’s answers at a time. Tell him the respondent’s name first (or “anonymous”), then walk through their answers, then he’ll ask if there’s another respondent. Each respondent becomes one row in the submitted survey log.

You can stop after one respondent if you only have one to enter. You can also come back later and add more respondents to the same log before submitting.

Cleaning schedules and other recurring logs

For logs like the FR2 Surgery Cleaning Specification, you’ll tell Benedict one task and date at a time. Say something like “Sinks and taps, done, today, my initials are SDA” — and Benedict will commit each task immediately.

Quick batch shortcut: say “today I did all of them” — Benedict will read the task list back to you for confirmation, then commit each one in a quick loop.

Reviewing or correcting after submission

Submitted a form and noticed a typo? Just tell Benedict: “Open the last Crown consent and change question 27.” He’ll open a fresh editable copy with that change applied. You can make more changes — ask for as many as you need before saying “submit.” The original submission stays in the audit trail; the new copy supersedes it.

When you’re done revising, say “submit” explicitly. Benedict will NOT auto-submit just because you confirmed a single change — he waits for an explicit submission cue.

Ending the session

When you’re done, say “that’s everything”, “thanks Benedict”, “goodbye”, or simply close the page. The cost meter shows your total for the session.

How Benedict charges you

Your bill runs on a single meter: teckel credits (tc). Every action Benedict takes against your data — reading your forms, writing an answer, opening the viewer, sending a submission, even just listening while you think — costs a small number of tc. The cost meter at the top of your Benedict page shows the running total in real time, with the pound/pence equivalent next to it. A typical session (filling a full compliance form, reviewing it, and submitting it) costs a few pence.

There are no separate per-call fees or hidden charges. What you see on the meter is what you pay. Long pauses cost very little — far less than active conversation — so feel free to think, look something up, or step away briefly without watching the clock.

Topping up your tc balance — the teckel App

teckel handles ALL your billing through tc. To buy credit, install the teckel App on your phone or tablet:

Sign into the App with the same wallet you use for Benedict, tap your credit balance, and choose a top-up amount. The purchase clears through the App’s in-app purchase flow (handled by Google or Apple, as appropriate). The credits land instantly in your teckel wallet and are usable from any Benedict session immediately.

If your balance gets low, Benedict will let you know before doing anything that would overdraw it. He won’t fail mid-form — he’ll pause and tell you to top up via the teckel App.

If you need the exact pricing for individual actions, ask Sabina or your practice administrator — the rates are set per item and shown live on the meter.

Five habits that keep costs down

1. Batch your answers when you can

Benedict can take a single sentence with many answers in it. Instead of saying “yes” · “yes” · “no” one at a time, try:

“Yes to questions 1, 2 and 4; no to question 3; the rest are all acknowledged.”

Benedict will batch-fill them with a single save_progress call instead of one tool call per answer. One save_progress is roughly the cost of three individual updates — and you’ll finish a 22-question form in about a minute instead of five.

You can do this at the start (give him the whole form), in the middle (skip ahead a block), or at the end (catch up the remaining ones).

2. Don’t ask to “see the form” more than once per state

When you ask Benedict to show you the form on screen, he mints a fresh view and the iframe loads it. If you then say “show me again” within the same minute, without any new data being entered, he’ll fire another full open-viewer cycle — which costs the same as the first time. (The system catches the obvious duplicates silently, but it’s worth knowing.)

The right uses of “show me the form”:

  • At the start of a session, to see what you’re filling.
  • After you’ve filled several answers and want to verify them visually.
  • Just before you submit, as a final review.

If nothing’s changed since the last view, you can trust what was there.

3. Don’t repeat yourself if Benedict heard you the first time

If Benedict acknowledges an answer (“Got it — that’s a yes for question seven”), it’s recorded. You don’t need to confirm it again unless he asks you to. Each “yes I did say yes” is another tool roundtrip.

If you genuinely want to change an answer, just tell him plainly: “Change question seven to no.” He’ll update it once.

4. Pause freely — silence is (almost) free

If you need to step away, take a phone call, or check a patient record, just stop talking. Long pauses cost very little — a 2-minute pause is roughly the cost of a few seconds of active conversation. When you come back, Benedict picks up where you left off — no need to apologise or recap.

This is the recommended pattern for any form where you need to look something up partway through.

5. Submit only once

When all your non-PII answers are in, Benedict will offer to submit. Say yes (or “submit it” / “send it”) and let the submission complete. If you re-ask “is it sent yet?” five times, each one is a tool call.

You’ll know it’s sent when:

  • Benedict says the recipient has been emailed.
  • The cost meter shows a submission line.
  • The form viewer shows the submitted state.

If you don’t hear that confirmation in 10–20 seconds, then it’s reasonable to ask once. Before that, just wait — submission is the longest single tool call (a PDF is generated, pinned to permanent storage, then emailed), so it can take a few seconds.

What’s already optimised for you

A few things the system handles automatically — you don’t need to do anything about them, but knowing they exist might save you a worried question:

  • PII fields can be left blank under GDPR. If a question asks for a patient name, date of birth, or anything else that identifies a person, you can say “skip” or simply not answer. Benedict will move on and the form will still submit cleanly.
  • The form viewer auto-heals. If the on-screen iframe ever stops refreshing, leave it for a few seconds — it refreshes its own access token in the background. You very rarely need to ask to “reopen” it.
  • The cost meter is always live. It starts ticking the moment you load the page (not when you start the conversation), and it shows the same currency at all scales: pence under £1, then pounds (with the residual pence still visible).
  • Confirmations are short. Benedict is trained to answer in one sentence when he can. If he’s giving you a long status report, it’s usually because you asked for one (“review my entries”, “what’s left”, etc.).

What costs the most (and how to spend it wisely)

Activity Relative cost Comment
Listening to you Lowest Pauses cost almost nothing
Acknowledging a single answer Low Each tick on the meter
Filling a single field Low–medium Bigger if the answer needs canonicalisation
Saving several answers in one go Medium Cheaper than the same answers individually
Opening the form viewer Medium Generates a fresh PDF preview
Submitting the form Highest PDF render + permanent storage + email
Generating a status report High Reads many records and produces a PDF

You’ll almost never see anything outside this range. If the meter jumps suddenly with no obvious cause, ask Sabina to take a look — it usually means something asked Benedict to repeat itself.

When to ask for help

  • Benedict didn’t hear you correctly: repeat once, more slowly. He’ll auto-correct most homophones.
  • An answer was recorded wrong: “Change question N to X.” Don’t try to undo the previous turn — just overwrite.
  • You’re stuck on a date format: Benedict will accept most natural phrasings (“ten May twenty-twenty-six”, “10th of May”, “10 May 2026”). If he keeps misinterpreting, spell out the year digits.
  • The form looks incomplete on screen: check whether the missing fields are PII (greyed out / not required) — they’re allowed to stay blank.
  • Benedict says “I haven’t loaded a form yet”: tell him which form you want by name. He’ll find it in the catalogue.
  • Anything else: ask Sabina. She’s the gatekeeper agent and can transfer you back to Benedict once whatever needed sorting is sorted.

A note on the cost meter

The meter shows what you’ve spent on this page load, for this voice agent, since you arrived. It does NOT show your account balance. If you ever need to top up, do that through your teckel wallet — Benedict will let you know if your balance dips below the cost of the action you’re about to take.

A single typical form submission (Q&A with around 20 questions, including a verbal walk-through and an email at the end) usually shows somewhere around a few pence on the meter. A longer log entry with multiple rows might run to 5–10p. Status reports and large grids can reach 15–20p. If yours is consistently higher, the most likely cause is repeating yourself — re-read habits 1 and 3.
